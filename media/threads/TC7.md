# TC7｜Thinking models — first principles, antifragility, and why Final thought — and I''ll die on this hill: systems beat goals. Every. Single. Time.

1/11
Three months. That''s how long I spent trying to squeeze another 2% out of my trading win rate. Three months I''ll never get back. Then I asked the question I''d been avoiding: "Should a 22-year-old with <10k capital be trading at all?"

Answer: no.

I''d skipped Step 1 of Musk''s 5-Step Algorithm — "question the requirement" — and jumped straight to Step 5: optimization. I''d say — and this is my actual opinion, not a tweet — this is the most common thinking error at 22.

2/11
First Principles, or as I now think of it: 'stop optimizing shit that doesn''t matter.' Break problems down to their irreducible elements. Rebuild from there.

My Life OS redesign: instead of optimizing "how to take better notes," I asked: "Is unverified knowledge an asset or a liability?" Answer: liability. So So I didn''t build a better note system — even though every productivity YouTuber told me to. Instead, I — I built "7 days unprocessed → auto-delete." First-principles-first, then design.

3/11
Musk''s 5-Step Algorithm. I''ve adapted it — less 'build rockets' more 'don''t be an idiot with your time':

1. Question the requirement — most skipped step
2. Delete — remove anything that doesn''t create value
3. Simplify — reduce to minimum viable structure
4. Accelerate — execute fast
5. Automate — ONLY after steps 1-4 are stable

Most people jump to Step 5. Automating a wrong process = efficiently producing wrong outputs faster.

4/11
The 5-Step audit for your current projects:

For each "habit/system/project" you''re running: how much time did you spend on Step 1 (questioning) vs Step 5 (automating)? If Step 1 time < Step 5 time → you''re probably optimizing something you shouldn''t be doing at all.

5/11
Antifragility (Taleb): not "resilience." Resilience = bounce back to original state. Antifragility = bounce back STRONGER than before.

Your muscles are antifragile — tear + rebuild = bigger. Your bones are antifragile — stress + recovery = denser. Your cognition can be antifragile — error + analysis + system upgrade = better thinker.

6/11
Counter-consensus: "I need a system that never breaks" is a fragile goal.

It assumes a perfect system exists. When it breaks (and it will), your belief in the system shatters too. The antifragile approach: accept that breakdowns WILL happen → design for faster recovery and system upgrades after each one.

System crashing isn''t the problem. Not rebooting is.

7/11
My Crash Recovery Protocol:

1. Recognize: "I''m in a crash" (awareness > everything)
2. Don''t judge: not "I failed again" → "system deviated, recovery mode activated"
3. Floor only: ≥6h sleep + 5min movement + 1 sentence + 1 meal
4. Day 2: floor again → Day 3: restore standard layer
5. Six-Grid Review → upgrade system (what environment design gap did this crash expose?)

From: "crash → self-punishment → deeper crash" → "crash → recovery → upgrade"

8/11
Systems > Goals — the most important mental model shift at 22:

Goals = what you write on paper: "I will lose 10kg." Every day until then = "haven''t achieved" = psychological failure.
Systems = what you do every day: "Did I exercise? Did I eat clean?" Yes → today SUCCEEDED.

Goals sit on paper. Systems walk the path. Focus on "is the system running?" not "am I there yet?"

9/11
Three Systems > Goals principles:

1. Track process metrics, not outcome metrics. "Did I write today?" (controllable) not "Did I gain 100 followers?" (uncontrollable).

2. System crashes auto-recover. Goal-based: "I''m 2 weeks behind → goal failed → give up." System-based: "Yesterday I didn''t write → today I write 1 sentence → system restored."

3. Don''t moralize system deviations. "Missed a day" ≠ "I''m a failure." = "System deviated. Recovering now."

10/11
Monthly System Audit (my actual practice):

5 core systems: Sleep / Exercise / Diet / Writing / Coding. Each month: what was each system''s uptime %? Systems <80% → this month''s focus (simplify baseline / change environment / lower expectations). Not "try harder." "Redesign so it''s harder to fail."

11/11
The meta-lesson after 2 years of building thinking models:

At 22, you don''t need more mental models. You need to apply the ones you already understand to ONE area of your life until you see measurable change. One model, one experiment, one result. Then the next. The gap isn''t "knowledge of models." It''s "models applied to real problems with real data."

Start with one. Question the requirement. Delete the noise. Build the system. Run it. Break it. Rebuild. Repeat.
